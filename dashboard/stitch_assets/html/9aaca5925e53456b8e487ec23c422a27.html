Chapter 3: Backpropagation and Gradient Descent

Dr. Alan Turing • Oct 24

In this section, we explore the mathematical foundations of the neural network layer, specifically focusing on how weights are updated during training to minimize the loss function. The core of modern deep learning relies on an elegant application of the chain rule from calculus, known as backpropagation.

Historical Context While the chain rule dates back to Leibniz, the specific application to neural networks (backpropagation) was popularized by Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams in 1986.

To understand gradient descent, we must first define our objective: finding a set of parameters $\theta$ that minimizes a given loss function $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$. The gradient, denoted as $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$, points in the direction of steepest ascent. Therefore, to minimize the loss, we take a step in the opposite direction.

$$ \theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \cdot \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta_t) $$

Where $\eta$ represents the learning rate, a crucial hyperparameter that determines the step size at each iteration. If $\eta$ is too large, the algorithm may overshoot the minimum and diverge. If it is too small, convergence will be painfully slow or it might get stuck in local minima.

As demonstrated above, navigating the loss landscape requires precise tuning. In a multi-dimensional space, calculating these gradients analytically for every weight becomes computationally infeasible. This is where the backpropagation algorithm shines, computing all necessary gradients efficiently in a single backward pass.
